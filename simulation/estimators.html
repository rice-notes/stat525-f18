Rao-Blackwellization

Consider some statistic θ\thetaθ that we compute over a specific chain. The estimate for that statistic on the posterior distribution can be written as a mean of that statisic over all chains:
θ^1sim=1S∑s=1Sθ1s \hat{\theta}^{\text{sim}}_1 = \frac{1}{S} \sum_{s=1}^S \theta^s_1 θ^1sim​=S1​s=1∑S​θ1s​

We can reduce the variance of this estimator if we use the conditional expectation of this estimator:
θ^1RB=1S∑s=1SE[θ1∣y,θ2=θ2s−1] \hat{\theta}^{\text{RB}}_1 = \frac{1}{S} \sum_{s=1}^S E\left[\theta_1 | y, \theta_2=\theta_2^{s-1} \right] θ^1RB​=S1​s=1∑S​E[θ1​∣y,θ2​=θ2s−1​]

RB Estimator

This is just the average of the conditional expectation of each of our parameters over all chains.

Conditional Expectations:

Comments

Consider transformations of variables, e.g. ψ=h(θ1)\psi = h(\theta_1)ψ=h(θ1​). It's straightforward to compute a simulation esimate for ψ\psiψ, just apply h(⋅)h(\cdot)h(⋅) to each sample of θ1\theta_1θ1​ and average. However, we might not have a simple conditional distribution for ψ\psiψ. In the above example, if we're interested in σ\sigmaσ rather than τ\tauτ, we can squint and see that we can use an Inverse-Gamma for σ\sigmaσ, but in general this will be challenging. For linear (can this be relaxed to monotonic?) functions, we can simply swap the expectation and the transformation, e.g. h(E[θ∣…])=E[h(θ)∣…]h(E[\theta | \ldots ]) = E[h(\theta) | \ldots ]h(E[θ∣…])=E[h(θ)∣…], but in general this is not the case.
