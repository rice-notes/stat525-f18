Example: Heart Transplant Data

Example: Poisson + Gamma

Consider the heart transplant data we've looked at before. Our goal here is to accurately model the rate of success of heart transplants for many hospitals jointly.

Data: For each hospital hhh: yh=# deaths within 30dxh=units of exposure (# of surgeries) \begin{aligned} y_h &= \text{\# deaths within 30d} \\ x_h &= \text{units of exposure (\# of surgeries)} \end{aligned} yh​xh​​=# deaths within 30d=units of exposure (# of surgeries)​

Model: yh∣λh∼Poisson(xhλh),h=1,…,H y_h | \lambda_h \sim \text{Poisson}(x_h\lambda_h), h = 1, \ldots, H yh​∣λh​∼Poisson(xh​λh​),h=1,…,H

Key Idea: Information from hospital lll (xl,λh)(x_l, \lambda_h)(xl​,λh​) may be informative for λh\lambda_hλh​.

Without Pooling

Model each {λh}h\{\lambda_h\}_h{λh​}h​ independently, e.g. λh∼Gamma(αh,βh)\lambda_h \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_h, \beta_h)λh​∼Gamma(αh​,βh​) or λ^h=yh/xh\hat{\lambda}_h = y_h / x_hλ^h​=yh​/xh​. We ignore the likely depedence between λi\lambda_iλi​'s, and λ^h\hat{\lambda}_hλ^h​ will be poor if xhx_hxh​ is small.

Complete Pooling

Fix λ1=λ2=…=λH=λ\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \ldots = \lambda_H = \lambdaλ1​=λ2​=…=λH​=λ. This ignores hospital-hospital variablity, and puts λ^=∑yh∑λh\hat{\lambda} = \frac{\sum y_h}{\sum \lambda_h}λ^=∑λh​∑yh​​.

Partial Pooling and Shrinkage Factors

Let [λh∣α,μ]∼Gamma(α,α/μ)[\lambda_h | \alpha, \mu] \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \alpha / \mu)[λh​∣α,μ]∼Gamma(α,α/μ). Prior mean μ\muμ, variance μ2/α\mu^2/\alphaμ2/α. Here, we share some information across hospitals, as we use the same prior distributions for each mortality rate.

We've seen this pairing before, and since the Gamma distribution is conjugate to the Poisson distribution, we get the following posterior:

[λh∣yh,α,μ]∼Gamma(α+yh,α/μ+xh) [\lambda_h | y_h, \alpha, \mu] \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha + y_h, \alpha / \mu + x_h) [λh​∣yh​,α,μ]∼Gamma(α+yh​,α/μ+xh​)

where the posterior mean is

E[λh∣yh,α,μ]=α+yhα/μ+xh=(1−kh)yhxh+khμkh=αα+xhμ∈[0,1] \begin{aligned} E[\lambda_h | y_h, \alpha, \mu] &= \frac{\alpha + y_h}{\alpha/\mu + x_h} \\ &= (1 - k_h)\frac{y_h}{x_h} + k_h \mu \\ k_h &= \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + x_h\mu} \in [0, 1] \end{aligned} E[λh​∣yh​,α,μ]kh​​=α/μ+xh​α+yh​​=(1−kh​)xh​yh​​+kh​μ=α+xh​μα​∈[0,1]​

So, we can see that α\alphaα is effectively a mixing parameter that controls our level of pooling. A high value of α\alphaα results in a khk_hkh​ of approximately 1, giving us complete pooling, or a single shared mean. A small value results in a khk_hkh​ of approximately 0, giving us no pooling, or completely independent means.
