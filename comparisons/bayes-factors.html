Bayes Factors

Bayes Factors

Goal: Compare two models (or hypotheses) H0H_0H0​ and H1H_1H1​:

H0:θ∈H0,H1:θ∈H1 H_0 : \theta \in H_0, H_1 : \theta \in H_1 H0​:θ∈H0​,H1​:θ∈H1​

H0H_0H0​ and H1=H∖H0H_1 = H \setminus H_0H1​=H∖H0​ are partitions of the parameter space HHH.

Prior Odds Ratio:

π0priorπ1prior=P(θ∈H0)P(θ∈H1)=∫H0P(θ)dθ∫H1P(θ)dθ) \frac{\pi_0^{\text{prior}}}{\pi_1^{\text{prior}}} = \frac{P(\theta \in H_0)}{ P(\theta \in H_1)} = \frac{\int_{H_0} P(\theta) d\theta}{ \int_{H_1} P(\theta) d\theta)} π1prior​π0prior​​=P(θ∈H1​)P(θ∈H0​)​=∫H1​​P(θ)dθ)∫H0​​P(θ)dθ​

Posterior Odds Ratio:

π0postπ1post=p(θ∈H0∣y)p(θ∈H1∣y)=∫H0p(θ∣y)dθ∫H1p(θ∣y)dθ)=∫H0p(y∣θ)p(θ)dθ∫H1p(y∣θ)p(θ)dθ) \frac{\pi_0^{\text{post}}}{\pi_1^{\text{post}}} = \frac{p(\theta \in H_0|y)}{ p(\theta \in H_1|y)} = \frac{\int_{H_0} p(\theta|y) d\theta}{ \int_{H_1} p(\theta|y) d\theta)} = \frac{\int_{H_0} p(y|\theta)p(\theta) d\theta}{ \int_{H_1} p(y|\theta)p(\theta) d\theta)} π1post​π0post​​=p(θ∈H1​∣y)p(θ∈H0​∣y)​=∫H1​​p(θ∣y)dθ)∫H0​​p(θ∣y)dθ​=∫H1​​p(y∣θ)p(θ)dθ)∫H0​​p(y∣θ)p(θ)dθ​

Bayes Factor: ratio of posterior odds to the prior odds

BF(H0,H1)=posterior oddsprior odds=π0prior/π1priorπ0post/π1post=p(y∣H0)p(y∣H1) BF(H_0, H_1) = \frac{\text{posterior odds}}{\text{prior odds}} = \frac{\pi_0^{\text{prior}} / \pi_1^{\text{prior}}}{\pi_0^{\text{post}} / \pi_1^{\text{post}}} = \frac{p(y|H_0)}{p(y|H_1)} BF(H0​,H1​)=prior oddsposterior odds​=π0post​/π1post​π0prior​/π1prior​​=p(y∣H1​)p(y∣H0​)​

Comments

  1. BF shows how the data update our prior beliefs of hypothesis
  2. general hypotheses are allowed
  3. can evaluate evidence in favor of a hypothesis; determine how much it contributes or doesn't
  4. can incorporate ouside information
  5. depends on marginal likelihood, generally difficult to compute, additionally marginal likelihoods can be very sensitive

Good Examples

  1. Suppose H0H_0H0​ and H1H_1H1​ have some parameterization with parameter θ∈H={θ0,θ1}\theta \in H = \{\theta_0, \theta_1\}θ∈H={θ0​,θ1​}, with H0:θ=θ0H_0 : \theta = \theta_0H0​:θ=θ0​ and H1:θ=θ1H_1 : \theta = \theta_1H1​:θ=θ1​. Then our Bayes Factor is simply the likelihood ratio given our parameter.

  2. One-sided Test of Normal Means: yi∣θ,σ∼N(θ,σ2)y_i | \theta, \sigma \sim N(\theta, \sigma^2)yi​∣θ,σ∼N(θ,σ2).

DIC

Idea: D(θ)D(\theta)D(θ) is a function of θ\thetaθ, so compute posterior expectation.

Dˉ=E[D(θ)∣y]≈1S∑sD(θs) \bar{D} = E[D(\theta)|y] \approx \frac{1}{S}\sum_s D(\theta^s) Dˉ=E[D(θ)∣y]≈S1​s∑​D(θs)

Modification: penalize D with model complexity. Two approaches:

Spiegelhalter (2002)

Let θ^\hat{\theta}θ^ be an estimator of θ∗=\theta^* =θ∗= true value. Put d(y,θ^,θ∗)=−2log⁡p(y∣θ∗)+2log⁡p(y∣θ^)=D(θ∗)−D(θ^) \begin{aligned} d(y, \hat{\theta}, \theta^*) &= -2\log{p(y|\theta^*)} + 2\log{p(y|\hat{\theta})} \\ &= D(\theta^*) - D(\hat{\theta}) \end{aligned} d(y,θ^,θ∗)​=−2logp(y∣θ∗)+2logp(y∣θ^)=D(θ∗)−D(θ^)​

Let θ^=E[θ∣y]\hat{\theta} = E[\theta | y]θ^=E[θ∣y]

pD=E[d(y,θ,θ^)∣y]=E[D(θ)−D(θ^)∣y]=Dˉ−D(θ^) p_D = E[d(y, \theta, \hat{\theta}) | y] = E[D(\theta) - D(\hat{\theta}) | y] = \bar{D} - D(\hat{\theta}) pD​=E[d(y,θ,θ^)∣y]=E[D(θ)−D(θ^)∣y]=Dˉ−D(θ^)

Intution: posterior mean of the deviance minus the deviance of the posterior mean.

Then, DIC=D(θ^)+2pD=Dˉ+pDDIC = D(\hat{\theta}) + 2p_D = \bar{D} + p_DDIC=D(θ^)+2pD​=Dˉ+pD​.

  • easy to compute
  • linear models w/ uniform priors: pD=pp_D = ppD​=p
  • but pDp_DpD​ can be negative!
